inMyProgrammingBackpack

Imagine starting a new project was like going to a trip or a mountain. What do you put in your backpack? In this post I am going to talk about my personal choices and also guide my-past-myself! to fast forward to today.

First thing I put in my backpack is a book, named 'Domain-Driven Design: Tackling Complexity in the Heart of Software', written by Eric Evans. I think I love and hate this book equally. I hate it because after reading this book I don't read technical books as much as I used to. I love it because it did answer many of my long-running questions.

Questions like: what should I name this function/method in this object/class? According to DDD(Domain-Driven Design) names should come out of a ubiquitous language, shaped by all people in project and especially Domain Experts. Therefore a new developer can gain domain knowledge by reading source code. How cool is that and I wish I knew this earlier.

I think this book is timeless, and can be useful for creating any long lasting software which is fun to extend and maintain. Isolating domain complexity in a layer and making this layer independent from other layers is a great idea. Personally I like the fact that Domain models are plain objects which this make it possible to write thousands of unit-test by total execution time as low as 2 seconds.

Second thing is my eBook-reader. I do have almost all books written by Martin Fowler in it, plus his signature series. What Martin Fowler is doing through his books and his website is amazing. I haven't seen nobody better than him in documenting his valuable experiences. My favourite book of him is 'Refactoring: Improving the Design of Existing Code'. I also do have books from other amazing authors such as Kent Beck. 'Test-Driven Development By Example' is one of his must read books.

Last things I put in my backpack is a bottle of water, an extra light/warm cloths, some fruits, a pencil and a notebook. that's all.
